# How to choose Swiss health insurance (LAMal/KVG): model, deductible and premium

## Introduction
Mandatory basic insurance (LAMal/KVG) covers the same legally defined benefits across insurers. What changes is:
- the **premium** (monthly price),
- the **access model** (how you enter the system: standard, GP/family doctor, telmed, HMO),
- and the practical **service** experience (billing clarity, support, apps, processes).

You don’t need to be an expert to choose well. You need to separate the decisions, understand your risk, and compare calmly.

## Context in Switzerland
Split the choice into three decisions:
1) insurer (price/service)  
2) model (standard vs GP vs telmed vs HMO)  
3) deductible (how much you pay before reimbursements)

Two concepts that often trip newcomers:
- **Basic vs complementary**: basic insurance (LAMal) is mandatory; complementary plans are optional and usually require acceptance.
- **Accidents**: if you’re employed and covered for accidents by your employer, you can typically exclude “accident” from basic insurance. If you’re not covered via employment, you should include it.

## Common situations that cause problems
### 1) Models: freedom vs discipline (where mistakes happen)
Models are the #1 source of surprises because they define **the rules for seeing a doctor**.

- **Standard**: most flexibility; often more expensive.
- **GP / family doctor** (*Hausarzt / médecin de famille*) model: your GP coordinates referrals; cheaper, but you must follow the pathway.
- **Telmed**: you call a medical hotline first; they advise and refer you. Cheaper, but requires discipline.
- **HMO / network**: you’re assigned to a medical centre/network. Often cheaper, but limits choice.

Typical mistake: picking telmed/HMO to save money, then bypassing the rule “because it was urgent” or “because I already know a specialist”. That can lead to reimbursement issues or billing disputes.

### 2) Deductible + cost sharing: your real cost is not just “12 premiums”
Your annual cost is usually more than “12 premiums”. Expect:
- a **deductible** (amount you pay first each year before reimbursements),
- **cost sharing** (a percentage after the deductible, up to a yearly cap),
- and potentially additional costs depending on the situation (e.g., hospital stays).

How to think about the deductible without complicated math:
- if you’re healthy and have a buffer, a higher deductible can make sense (lower premium, higher one‑off risk),
- if you have predictable medical expenses, a lower deductible reduces uncertainty.

Simple example (indicative):
Person A rarely uses healthcare → often prefers lower premiums + higher deductible.  
Person B has regular follow‑ups → often prefers predictability.

### 3) Complementary insurance: why “later” can be a bad plan
Complementary plans (often called LCA) can cover things the basic plan typically doesn’t cover well (depending on the policy): dental, glasses, private/semi‑private hospital rooms, etc.

Typical mistake: “I’ll buy it when I need it”. Complementary plans often involve:
- a medical questionnaire,
- possible rejection,
- or exclusions.

If you think you’ll want them, compare early.

### 4) Switching each year: deadlines and proof
Switching is usually prepared in autumn and involves deadlines. Practical translation:
- compare early,
- don’t wait until the last day,
- keep written confirmations.

Typical mistake: deciding at the last minute and not being able to prove the cancellation arrived in time.

## How to avoid fines or conflicts
### A step-by-step checklist
1) Define your situation: employed/self‑employed/student, family, usual doctor, health needs.  
2) Choose a model you will **actually follow** in real life.  
3) Select a deductible based on risk and buffer (not only the premium).  
4) Check whether accident cover should be included in basic insurance.  
5) Compare offers in the official comparator.  
6) Document everything: emails, confirmations, dates.

### How to compare in 15 minutes (a method that works)
1) Filter by your place of residence (premiums vary by region).  
2) Keep the same model and deductible while comparing (apples to apples).  
3) Look at the yearly cost, not just the cheapest month.  
4) Check operational constraints: hotline hours (telmed), assigned centres (HMO), etc.  
5) When in doubt, pick the option with rules you can reliably follow and good support — you don’t want process friction when you’re sick.

### A coherent decision (quick examples)
- Healthy, disciplined, employed → telmed + higher deductible can be coherent.  
- Kids or frequent visits → GP model + lower deductible often reduces stress.  
- Need flexibility → standard may be worth the price.

## Practical tips
### Common newcomer pitfalls
- “Coverage is the same” does not mean “the process is the same”: the model matters.
- Don’t optimise for the first month; think in annual cost and risk.
- When in doubt, ask insurers in writing (you keep a record).

### Mini glossary
- **LAMal/KVG**: mandatory basic insurance.
- **Premium**: monthly payment.
- **Deductible**: yearly amount you pay first.
- **Cost sharing**: what you pay after the deductible (up to a cap).
- **Telmed/HMO/GP**: models with different rules.

### If you move within Switzerland (budget detail people miss)
Premiums depend on your region. If you move canton/municipality, your premium can change too. Practical advice:
- inform your insurer as soon as your address is confirmed,
- check whether your chosen model (HMO/network) still has convenient centres near your new home,
- and re‑do your annual budget (not only the cheapest month).
