# RAV and unemployment in Switzerland: how it works, steps and obligations

## Introduction
Unemployment in Switzerland is not “just a payment”: it’s a structured system with **rules, deadlines and evidence**. If you run it with a method (documentation + calendar + written communication), it tends to be predictable. If you improvise, you’ll likely face delays, back‑and‑forth with the fund, or sanctions.

## Context in Switzerland
You will usually deal with two entities (plus one key idea):

- **RAV / ORP** (*Office régional de placement*): assigns you an advisor, sets job‑search expectations, schedules appointments and may offer “measures” (courses, reintegration programmes, etc.).
- **Unemployment fund** (*Arbeitslosenkasse / caisse de chômage*): checks eligibility, calculates your benefit and **pays** it.

Key idea: the system runs on documentation. Saying “I searched” is not enough — you must be able to prove it.

The legal framework is federal (ALV/AC), but practical implementation can vary by canton (appointment rhythm, forms, what is considered “reasonable”, etc.).

## Common situations that cause problems
### What the RAV is (and what it isn’t)
The RAV is not a classic “placement agency” that finds a job for you. Its role is to:
- confirm you are available for work,
- structure your job search (appointments, goals),
- and record your process (measures, applications, evidence).

In practice, **you** remain the driver of the job search. The RAV provides the framework.

### Who is usually eligible (practical view)
Eligibility is case‑dependent, but it typically revolves around:
- having a sufficient **contribution period** within the reference window,
- being unemployed and **available** to work,
- being **registered** and complying with obligations (appointments, documented job search),
- and not being in a situation that triggers sanctions or exclusions (for example, certain resignations without an accepted reason can lead to “no‑pay” days).

For newcomers, the sensitive point is often proving contributions and demonstrating real availability (language, permits, realistic job options). It’s not moral judgement — it’s eligibility and employability.

### How much you get (indicative)
Benefits are calculated on your **insured salary** and often expressed as a percentage. As a general rule:
- many people receive roughly **70%** of the insured salary, and
- roughly **80%** in certain cases (for example, family dependents or specific situations).

This is not a “guess”: the fund calculates it based on your documents. Missing documents often mean delayed payments or later recalculations.

### Duration: compensated days, not “months”
Duration is often expressed in **daily allowances** (for example, 200/260/400/520), depending on factors such as:
- contribution history,
- age,
- and conditions of the case.

Practical translation: two people who become unemployed on the same day may have different durations. The system is managed in compensated days rather than exact calendar months.

### Obligations (and where sanctions actually happen)
Most problems come from lack of system, not lack of motivation. Typical obligations include:
- **provable job search** (real applications, relevant roles, evidence),
- attending **appointments** and measures,
- accepting **reasonable** measures/offers (depending on profile and phase),
- submitting documents **on time**.

Common sanction triggers:
- missed deadlines (late submissions),
- no evidence kept (emails/screenshots/job ad),
- resigning without advice (can lead to sanctions),
- “filler applications” (not aligned with your profile or without evidence).

### A realistic example
Case A: “My contract ends on the 31st. I register on the 15th next month and only start keeping evidence from then.” Typical result: friction, lost days or delays.

Case B: “I register early and from day one I keep a tracker + evidence.” Typical result: smoother process and fewer questions.

## How to avoid fines or conflicts
### Minimum checklist (documentation)
Prepare a (digital) folder with:
1) ID + permit (if applicable)  
2) contracts, termination letters, payslips  
3) relevant certificates (depending on your case)  
4) application “evidence pack”

### How to document applications without going crazy
A simple spreadsheet is enough. Useful columns:
- date sent
- company and role
- channel (portal/email/referral)
- evidence (link to pdf/screenshot/email)
- status/response

Minimum evidence per application:
- job ad or role description (PDF/screenshot),
- the CV/cover letter you sent,
- confirmation (email or portal screenshot).

### Communication: in writing, and ahead of deadlines
If they request something, reply in writing and **before** the deadline. If you hand in documents in person, keep a receipt if one exists. In Switzerland, when there’s disagreement, the documented record wins.

## Practical tips
### Advice for newcomers
- Don’t assume it works like in your home country: here it’s very **documentation‑driven**.
- If your local language is limited, say it early and keep the job search realistic (companies, sectors, area). You must search — the method must be viable.
- If you are considering resigning, get advice first: a “pride decision” can cost you weeks without payments.

### A weekly routine that usually works
1) fixed job‑search blocks (treat it like work)  
2) fewer, higher‑quality applications  
3) save evidence immediately (not “tomorrow”)  
4) a calendar for appointments and deadlines

### Red flag
If you keep “reconstructing” applications later, you’re already behind. Simplify: auto screenshots, a monthly folder, and a tracker kept up to date.
