# RAV/ORP and unemployment in Switzerland (ALV/AC): how it works, eligibility and how to avoid sanctions

## Introduction
In Switzerland, unemployment is not only “getting benefits”. It’s a system built around **deadlines**, **documentation**, and **obligations**. If you run it with a method (calendar + proof + written communication), it’s often predictable. If you improvise, you risk delays, blocked payments, or **sanctions** (*Einstelltage* / jours de suspension).

This guide explains, practically, what the **RAV/ORP** does, what the **unemployment fund** does, how benefits are calculated (indicative), and the mistakes newcomers make most often.

## How the system works
In most cases, two actors are involved (plus one key idea):

### RAV / ORP (placement and monitoring)
The **RAV** (*Regionales Arbeitsvermittlungszentrum*) / **ORP** (*Office régional de placement*) is your reintegration framework:
- you get an advisor,
- you receive job‑search targets,
- you’re called to appointments,
- and you may be assigned measures (courses, programs, etc.).

### Unemployment fund (calculation and payment)
The **unemployment fund** (*Arbeitslosenkasse / caisse de chômage*) is the entity that:
- confirms eligibility,
- calculates the amount,
- and **pays** the benefit.

### Key idea: what you can’t prove doesn’t exist
Much of the file is evidence‑driven:
- job applications with proof,
- documents delivered on time,
- written confirmations.

The legal basis is federal (ALV/AC), but day‑to‑day operations can vary by canton (appointment rhythm, forms, practical expectations).

## Requirements or rules
### 1) When to register (practical rule)
If you’re dismissed or your contract ends, act early:
- register without waiting “to see what happens”,
- and document your job search from day 1.

Delays can cost money or days of entitlement.

### 2) Who is usually eligible (practical view)
Eligibility depends on your case, but typically relates to:
- having contributed long enough in a reference period,
- being unemployed and **available** to work,
- being registered and meeting obligations,
- and not being in a sanction‑triggering situation (for example, certain resignations without recognised reasons).

For newcomers, sensitive points are often:
- proving contributions (Switzerland and/or coordination under agreements),
- and proving real availability (language, permits, mobility, etc.).

### 3) How much you receive (indicative) and the “cap”
Benefits are calculated based on **insured salary**, often expressed as a percentage:
- commonly around **70%**,
- and around **80%** in certain cases (e.g., dependants, specific situations).

There is also a **maximum insured salary**: above that, the calculation is capped. A commonly cited reference is around **CHF 148,200/year** (≈ **CHF 12,350/month**) as a maximum insured salary, but you should verify the current value in official sources.

### 4) Duration: compensated days, not “months”
Duration is usually expressed in **compensated days** (e.g., 200/260/400/520) depending on:
- contribution history,
- age,
- and file conditions.

Practical translation: two people entering unemployment in the same month may have different durations.

### 5) Typical obligations (and where sanctions come from)
Common obligations include:
- demonstrable job search (real, suitable applications with proof),
- attending appointments and measures,
- accepting “reasonable” measures/offers for your profile,
- delivering documentation on time.

Sanctions often come from:
- missed deadlines,
- missing evidence,
- resigning without advice,
- “filler applications” with no logic or proof.

### 6) Documents you’ll typically be asked for
The exact list depends on your case, but prepare a “base pack”:
- termination letter / end‑of‑contract letter (with dates),
- employment contract(s) and recent payslips,
- bank details (for payments),
- residence/permit documents if relevant,
- and any document explaining special situations (training, sickness, reduced capacity, etc.).

Tip: for important submissions, keep proof of submission/receipt (email, portal confirmation, registered mail).

### 7) “Reasonable” offers and measures (how to approach it)
What counts as “reasonable” can feel stressful. In practice, the system expects:
- coherent search aligned with your profile,
- and increasing flexibility over time if results don’t come.

This goes smoother if you align early with your advisor and document your strategy (target roles, region, languages, contract type).

## Common mistakes
1) Registering late (“I’ll find something quickly”).  
2) Not saving proof (ad screenshots, PDFs, sent emails, portal confirmations).  
3) Reconstructing applications at month‑end (when proof is missing).  
4) Treating the RAV as a “job agency”: you are the engine; RAV provides structure and control.  
5) Confusing RAV vs fund responsibilities: one schedules you, the other pays.  
6) Resigning without advice: it can trigger significant sanctions; check before you do it.  
7) Mixing up “appointments” and “document deadlines”: both matter; missing either can have consequences.  

## Practical examples
### Example 1 — “One day late” can be costly
Realistic scenario: you’re asked to submit a document by date X and you submit it the next day. In Switzerland, that kind of delay can translate into financial loss (suspension days or non‑recognition). It’s not personal; it’s rule‑based.

How to avoid it:
- submit **before** the deadline,
- keep proof of submission/receipt.

### Example 2 — Documenting applications without going crazy
A minimal system:
- a spreadsheet with date, company, role, channel, status, link to proof.
- per application, save: the ad, what you sent, and a confirmation.

File structure that works:
- one folder per month (e.g., `2026-03`),
- filenames like `YYYY-MM-DD_Company_Role_(ad|cv|proof).pdf/png`.

### Example 3 — Understanding the insured salary cap
If your salary was below the cap, the calculation often uses your full salary. If it was above, it’s capped.
Practical translation: you don’t receive “70% of any salary”, but 70/80% of insured salary **up to the cap**.

### Example 4 — Resignation vs dismissal (why it matters)
Dismissal: typically less friction if you meet requirements.  
Resignation: even with reasons, you may face suspension days if the reason isn’t recognised or documented properly.

Practical takeaway: before resigning, get advice and keep a written trail so you don’t “buy yourself” weeks without payment.

## Useful advice
### Day‑1 checklist (when you’re dismissed)
1) Get the termination letter and confirm dates in writing.  
2) Create a digital folder and save everything (contract, payslips, letters).  
3) Register following cantonal procedure.  
4) Start an application tracker immediately.  
5) Set deadline reminders (documents, appointments, submissions).  

### A weekly routine that often works
- Fixed job‑search blocks (treat it like work).  
- Fewer, better‑targeted applications.  
- Save evidence the same day.  
- Prepare 6–8 STAR stories for interviews.  

### If you work temp/part‑time while registered
Temporary work (*Zwischenverdienst*) can be positive: structure, income, references. Key points:
- keep meeting RAV obligations,
- document contract/hour changes,
- clarify with the fund how it affects payments.

### What can vary by canton (and how to handle it)
- appointment rhythm and forms,
- practical expectations for “sufficient” proof,
- submission channels (portal, email, in‑person).

How to handle it: ask “what proof do you need for this to be accepted?” and save the answer.

### Mini glossary
- **RAV/ORP**: placement office and monitoring.  
- **Fund**: calculates and pays.  
- **ALV/AC**: unemployment insurance.  
- **Einstelltage / suspension days**: days without payment due to sanctions.
