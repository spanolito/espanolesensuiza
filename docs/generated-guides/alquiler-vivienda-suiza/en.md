# Renting and housing in Switzerland: application dossier, deposit, contract, inspections and moving out safely

## Introduction
Renting in Switzerland surprises newcomers for two main reasons: **competition** and **paperwork**. The upside is that once you understand the sequence (dossier → contract → move‑in condition report → house rules → move‑out inspection), you drastically reduce your risk of:
- losing apartments because your dossier is weak,
- getting scammed due to urgency,
- or paying unfair “move‑out charges” for pre‑existing defects.

This guide is Switzerland‑wide. Some details can vary by canton, municipality, or building rules.

## How the system works
Most rentals are managed by:
- an agency/management company (*régie* is common in French‑speaking Switzerland),
- or directly by the owner (less common in cities).

Typical flow:
1) search and viewings (often group viewings),  
2) application with a complete **dossier**,  
3) selection (often many candidates),  
4) contract signature,  
5) keys + **move‑in condition report**,  
6) living in the apartment (building rules),  
7) move‑out + final inspection.

Key idea: the most important document isn’t the listing, it’s the **move‑in condition report**. It protects you later.

## Requirements or rules
### 1) The dossier (your “tenant CV”)
A strong dossier often includes:
- passport/ID and, if relevant, your permit or proof of application,
- employment contract or proof of income,
- a short cover letter (who you are, move‑in date, stability),
- and, if you already live in Switzerland, a **debt register extract** (*Betreibungsauszug*).

If you’re new and can’t provide the debt extract yet:
- say it clearly,
- compensate with stability proof (contract, income, savings, references).

### 2) The real price: base rent vs charges (Nebenkosten)
Listings often separate:
- **net rent** (base),
- **ancillary charges** (*Nebenkosten*: heating/water/common costs), sometimes as an advance later adjusted.

Ask what’s included and how adjustments work. A “cheap” apartment can be expensive if charges are high.

### 3) Deposit: how to do it safely
Residential deposits are common (often up to three months of base rent).

Practical safety rule:
- the deposit should be handled in a **traceable** way (ideally a blocked deposit account in your name),
- avoid transfers to personal accounts without clear documentation,
- keep everything (contract, receipts, release conditions).

### 4) The contract: three items you must read
1) **Notice period** (how and when you can terminate).  
2) Rent + charges and what’s included.  
3) Special rules: subletting, pets, painting/renovations.

If something matters (e.g., “they will fix X before move‑in”), get it in writing.

### 5) Move‑in: the condition report (état des lieux)
At move‑in:
- inspect walls, floors, kitchen, bathroom, windows, appliances,
- record existing defects and take photos,
- sign the report and keep a copy.

This is what determines what you can be charged for at move‑out.

### 6) Inspections and landlord access
Common question: “can the agency come to inspect ‘just because’?”
In general, access is tied to a **justified reason** (repairs, checking damage, showing the flat to future tenants/buyers). A purely periodic “general inspection” without reason is typically not an automatic right, even if some management companies try.

Practical translation:
- ask for the reason and proposed date in writing,
- propose reasonable alternatives if needed,
- avoid refusing without offering options (to reduce conflict).

### 7) Repairs, defects and communication with the agency
During the tenancy, defects can appear (heating, appliances, humidity). Practical rule:
- report defects **in writing** (email) as early as possible,
- describe the issue and attach photos,
- keep the replies.

Avoid major “DIY repairs” without approval: later you may face disputes about costs and responsibility.

### 8) Terminating the lease: notice, dates and replacement tenants
Ending a lease in Switzerland is often a small project:
- check the **notice period** and official termination dates,
- send termination in the required form (when in doubt: registered letter),
- keep proof of sending.

If you need to leave early, many situations are handled by proposing a **replacement tenant** (*Nachmieter*) who is reasonable/solvent. Exact rules depend on contract and canton, but the practical idea is: it’s not enough to “move out”; you must manage the replacement properly.

## Common mistakes
- Applying with an incomplete dossier.
- Paying a “reservation” without a verifiable contract (scam signal).
- Budgeting without separating base rent and charges.
- Not documenting condition at move‑in.
- Trusting verbal promises.
- Not understanding “normal wear” vs damage at move‑out.
- Leaving early without following the notice/replacement process and paying extra months.

## Practical examples
### Example 1 — “No viewing, pay today” (classic scam pattern)
Red flags:
- no viewing possible,
- price too good,
- artificial urgency to transfer money,
- unclear identity.

Rule: if you can’t verify apartment + person + contract, don’t transfer.

### Example 2 — Move‑out charges: what to discuss
The final inspection often determines whether you get your deposit back.
Typical disputes:
- holes from pictures,
- wear marks,
- “cleaning not accepted”,
- pre‑existing defects.

Method:
1) compare with the move‑in report,  
2) use dated photos,  
3) separate normal wear from damage,  
4) request invoices/justification for large charges.

### Example 3 — Access for repairs
If repairs are needed, access is usually justified. Reasonable approach:
- agree on a time,
- confirm who will enter (technician, management),
- keep a written record.

### Example 4 — Early exit with a replacement tenant (without drama)
If you move for work or family and can’t reach the contractual date:
1) review the contract and termination dates,  
2) find a strong candidate (complete dossier),  
3) submit the candidate properly to the agency,  
4) keep all replies in writing.  

### Example 5 — Deposit dispute
If you’re charged for items you believe are unfair:
- request a breakdown and invoices,
- use the move‑in report + dated photos,
- if needed, check your canton’s conciliation authority (typical channel for rental disputes).

## Useful advice
### Application checklist (fast)
- One PDF with: ID + income + letter + attachments.
- Short email text (move‑in date, contact, availability).
- An application tracker.

### Move‑in checklist
- Condition report + photos.
- Meter readings if relevant.
- Confirm keys, mailbox, storage, parking.

### Move‑out checklist (protect your deposit)
- Deep clean (or a cleaning company if needed).
- Fix obvious items that are your responsibility.
- Photos on the move‑out day.
- Bring the move‑in report copy.

### Where to get help (without losing weeks)
- Tenant associations (ASLOCA / Mieterverband).
- Cantonal conciliation authorities/services (depending on canton).

For serious issues, getting help early is usually cheaper than “waiting”.

### What varies by canton/municipality/building
- market pressure (competition),
- management practices,
- recycling/noise/laundry rules,
- and support services/tenant associations.
