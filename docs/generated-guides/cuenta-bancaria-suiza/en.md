# Opening a bank account in Switzerland: CH‑IBAN, requirements and common mistakes

## Introduction
Opening a Swiss bank account sounds simple… until you try to do it “from abroad” or without the right paperwork. Many newcomers are surprised by two things:
- **before arrival**, most banks won’t open an account (even with an employment contract),
- and **after arrival**, a **CH‑IBAN** is often the key to smooth admin (salary, bills, official payments).

This guide explains how it works in practice, what banks typically request, and how to avoid the time‑wasting mistakes.

## How the system works
Swiss banking is compliance‑heavy: identity, domicile and traceability matter. After years of international pressure, many banks apply strict onboarding checks.

In practice you’ll see two “worlds”:
- **Swiss banks** (CH‑IBAN, good fit for payroll/bills, TWINT, but often with fees),
- **fintech/neobanks** (great for travel and transfers, sometimes with non‑Swiss IBAN and limitations for local admin).

Key idea: for day‑to‑day integration, the usual goal is **a bank account with an IBAN starting with CH**.

## Requirements or rules
### 1) Why “opening from abroad” usually fails
Common question: “I’m moving to Switzerland—can I open the account now to receive salary?”  
Practical answer: in most cases, **no**.

Typical reasons:
- the bank wants a verifiable Swiss **address**,
- depending on the bank, a **permit** or administrative confirmation,
- plus internal risk/compliance checks.

### 2) What documents are commonly requested
It varies, but expect:
- passport/ID,
- proof of address (rental contract, residence confirmation),
- and depending on your case: employment contract or proof of activity/income.

Tip: bring both PDF and paper copies. Avoid partial screenshots or unsigned contracts.

### 3) CH‑IBAN: why it matters
In Switzerland, CH‑IBAN makes it easier to:
- receive salary from employers,
- pay bills and set up recurring payments,
- access services that expect a Swiss account.

If you already use Revolut/Wise/N26, they can still be useful—but often as a **secondary** account.

### 3.1) Special case: rental deposit account
For renting, it’s common that the deposit is placed into a **blocked deposit account** in your name (rather than a landlord’s personal account). The practical idea is:
- the funds are “locked” as security,
- and release typically requires agreement or a formal process.

If you plan to rent, ask how the deposit is handled and what documents the bank needs.

### 4) Fees: the detail people don’t budget for
It’s common to pay:
- a monthly fee,
- per‑operation fees,
- or meet conditions (minimum balance, package bundles, etc.).

It’s not necessarily “bad”, but it’s part of your cost of living. Read the conditions before signing.

### 5) Account types: personal, joint and “salary setup”
Depending on your situation, you may want:
- a **personal account** (the baseline),
- a **joint account** (couples/families for shared bills),
- or a separate “fixed costs” account to control your budget.

Always ask:
- what’s included (card, payments, transfers),
- whether there are limits for withdrawals or foreign payments,
- and what conditions trigger extra fees.

### 6) Switching/closing later: plan the exit
Opening matters, but being able to **switch** later matters too:
- are there closing fees or lock‑ins?
- how do you move recurring payments?
- do they provide statements/certificates easily?

Planning the exit prevents you from being stuck with a bad setup out of inertia.

Practical trick: during a switch, keep the old account open for 1–2 months so delayed charges (subscriptions, quarterly bills) still clear. It helps you avoid overdrafts and disputes.

## Common mistakes
- Relying only on a foreign IBAN for payroll/official admin: it may work… until it doesn’t.
- Trying to open without an address and getting stuck: anchor your file (commune/address) first.
- Ignoring fees: they add up over a year.
- Taking the first offer without comparing: cheap upfront can be expensive in constraints/fees.
- Mixing personal and “project” money (self‑employment, rentals) without structure.

## Practical examples
### Example 1 — You need your first salary paid next month
A realistic method:
1) secure an address and register with the commune,  
2) use that confirmation + your contract at the bank,  
3) get a CH‑IBAN and card,  
4) update HR with the details.

### Example 2 — You want TWINT and local payments
TWINT is common for peer‑to‑peer payments. Some setups require a Swiss bank integration. If you need it, prioritise a compatible Swiss account.

### Example 3 — You need a certificate or proof of account
Landlords, employers or authorities may ask for a simple document showing your bank details (IBAN, holder name). A good bank/app should let you download statements and basic confirmations without drama.

## Useful advice
### A simple bank selection checklist
- Do I get a **CH‑IBAN**?
- What’s the monthly cost and what’s included (card, cash withdrawals, transfers)?
- Can I do everything in an app (statements, certificates, support)?
- How does onboarding work (appointment, video ID, timelines)?
- How easy is it to close or switch later?

### How to prepare the opening in 30 minutes (and avoid two trips)
Before you go:
- bring ID + rental contract (or address confirmation) + employment contract (if relevant),
- prepare your questions (fees, CH‑IBAN, TWINT, cards),
- and make sure your phone is charged (many steps run via apps).

After opening:
- save the contract and conditions (PDF),
- test a transfer and download your first statement,
- update HR and bill payees as soon as possible.

### Organisation: two accounts often beat one
Many people do well with:
- one Swiss “main” account (salary/bills),
- one fintech account for travel and FX.

### Security rule
Never share codes, PINs or access by phone. If someone calls “from the bank” with urgency, hang up and call back using the official number. Bank scams exist and can be sophisticated.
