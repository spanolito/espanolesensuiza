# First steps after arriving in Switzerland: an administrative checklist (without getting lost)

## Introduction
The first days in Switzerland are a mix of excitement and paperwork: address, registration, health insurance, work, banking, bills… and a lot of new acronyms. The usual problem is not “doing it”, but **doing it in the right order** and keeping proof.

This guide is a landing map for newcomers. It doesn’t replace your canton/commune’s specific rules, but it gives you a method that avoids the most common mistakes.

## How the system works
Switzerland is federal: many rules are national, but day‑to‑day execution is **cantonal and communal**. That’s why:
- your **commune/municipality** is often your first contact point (resident registration),
- and your canton handles several parts of the file (permit, some tax aspects, etc.).

Key idea: administration runs on **documents and deadlines**. Save confirmations, emails, letters and screenshots.

## Requirements or rules
### 1) A real address (your “anchor”)
Many steps require a real, verifiable address. Sleeping in an Airbnb may work for a few nights, but it may not be sufficient for registration or proof of domicile depending on the situation.

If you’re between accommodations, ask before assuming you can register with a temporary address.

What usually works best as “provable domicile”:
- a rental contract in your name (or an equivalent document),
- a formal confirmation from the landlord/host,
- or a recognised accommodation document (depending on the commune).

If you only have a “borrowed” address, the risk is missing official mail or being asked for additional proof later.

### 2) Register with the commune (as early as possible)
One of the first steps is usually **commune registration**. That opens your local file and unlocks other processes.

What communes often request:
- passport/ID,
- rental contract or proof of address,
- and, if relevant, an employment contract or proof of your purpose of stay.

Practical tip: bring both a **printed** and a **digital** (PDF) version. If something is missing, ask what they accept as a substitute and, if possible, get the answer in writing.

### 3) Mandatory health insurance (LAMal/KVG): don’t leave it for “later”
Basic insurance is mandatory. Details depend on your case, but the practical rule is:
- don’t wait until the last moment,
- choose a coherent model/deductible,
- and keep written confirmations of enrolment.

Treat LAMal as a yearly decision: if you pick a model you won’t follow (telmed/HMO without discipline), you’ll face issues when you’re sick. If you pick a very high deductible without a buffer, the “premium savings” can backfire.

### 4) A Swiss bank account (CH‑IBAN)
To receive salary, pay bills and operate normally, having a **Swiss CH‑IBAN** avoids a lot of friction. Some international fintech options work, but for payroll and official payments, CH‑IBAN is often the safest baseline.

Expect the “Swiss standard” checklist: identity + address + sometimes proof of status (permit or a confirmation). If you don’t have everything yet, ask what they accept (for example, a commune registration confirmation).

### 5) AHV/AVS number and payroll basics
AHV/AVS is the core social security system. If you are employed, the employer handles many registrations, but it’s still important to know:
- you have an AHV/AVS number,
- payroll includes deductions,
- and your personal data must be correct (name, date of birth, address).

Month‑1 tip: check that your name and date of birth match across documents (commune, bank, insurer). A small typo can trigger returned mail or silly administrative blocks.

## Common mistakes
- Doing things “by feel” without proof: later you can’t demonstrate submissions or enrolments.
- Using a non‑verifiable address (temporary accommodation that doesn’t work for all steps).
- Leaving LAMal for the last week: rushing leads to a bad yearly choice.
- Not organising documents: in Switzerland, a lost document can cost a week.
- Mixing up “urgent” and “important”: urgent is often housing/registration; important is closing each step with proof.
- Signing without reading: phone/internet contracts, complementary insurance, etc. Avoid unnecessary commitments in month 1.

## Practical examples
### Example 1 — You arrive with a job contract but temporary housing
Typical case: you have a contract, but you stay 3–4 weeks in temporary housing. Instead of improvising:
1) check with the commune whether the address works for registration,  
2) if not, prioritise a registrable address,  
3) and prepare your dossier (documents, insurance, banking) in parallel.

### Example 2 — Two adults, different timelines
In a couple, one starts working while the other searches. A simple method:
- a shared folder with documents,
- a deadline calendar,
- and a clear dependency list (address → commune → insurance/banking).

### Example 3 — You’re missing one document and everything feels blocked
Realistic situation: you’re asked for a document you can’t obtain yet (because you just arrived). Instead of stopping:
1) ask what alternatives they accept,  
2) submit what you do have (so your file exists),  
3) confirm the deadline for the remaining item.

In Switzerland, many problems are solved by “submitting something verifiable” instead of leaving the file at zero.

## Useful advice
### A 10‑point checklist (recommended order)
1) Secure an **address** you can prove.  
2) Gather core documents: ID, contracts, certificates.  
3) Register with the **commune** early.  
4) Start/choose **LAMal/KVG** (model + deductible).  
5) Open a **CH bank account** (if needed for payroll/bills).  
6) Review your first **payslip** and deductions (if employed).  
7) Build your system: folder + filenames + dates.  
8) Activate essentials (phone/internet) with clear contracts.  
9) Save useful contacts (commune, insurer, employer, doctor).  
10) After ~30 days: check what’s missing (letters, confirmations).

### A file system that saves hours
Suggested folders:
- `01_id_and_permits`
- `02_commune_registration`
- `03_health_lamal`
- `04_work_payslips`
- `05_bank_bills`

Always save the date, confirmation and a PDF copy.

### Golden rule
If a step can affect money or your right to live/work here, use three habits:
- confirm key points in writing,
- keep proof of submission/receipt,
- and set reminders for deadlines.
