# Swiss health insurance (LAMal/KVG): choosing a model and deductible without expensive mistakes

## Introduction
In Switzerland, mandatory basic health insurance (LAMal/KVG) is required. What confuses newcomers is:
- legally defined benefits are the same across insurers,
- but your real‑life experience and yearly cost change with the **premium**, the **access model**, and the **deductible/cost sharing**.

This guide shows a practical method to choose well, with examples, and notes what can vary by canton (premiums, subsidies, network availability).

## How the system works
### 1) Basic vs supplementary insurance
- **Basic (LAMal/KVG)**: mandatory. Benefits are defined by law.
- **Supplementary (VVG/LCA)**: optional. Often requires medical questionnaires; acceptance is not guaranteed.

### 2) Three separate decisions (why people get it wrong)
Separate:
1) **Insurer** (price/service)  
2) **Model** (standard / GP / telmed / HMO-network)  
3) **Deductible** (what you pay first each year)

### 3) Accidents: the detail that changes the premium
If you’re an employee covered for accidents by your employer (depending on conditions), you can often exclude “accident” from basic insurance. If you’re not covered via an employer (self‑employed, not working, etc.), you usually include it.

## Requirements or rules
### 1) Access models (where surprises come from)
Models define **how you enter the system**:
- **Standard**: most freedom, often more expensive.
- **GP/family doctor**: you coordinate through your GP and follow referrals.
- **Telmed**: you call a medical hotline first and follow their pathway.
- **HMO/network**: you’re tied to a centre/network.

Common mistake: choosing telmed/HMO to save money, then bypassing the pathway because “it was urgent” or “I already know the specialist”. That can lead to reimbursement issues or disputes.

### 2) Deductible and cost sharing: your yearly cost is not “12 premiums”
Budget for:
- **monthly premium**,
- **deductible** (annual amount you pay first),
- **cost sharing** after the deductible up to a cap,
- and potential additional contributions (e.g., hospital contributions).

Practical rule:
- healthy + buffer → higher deductible can fit,
- predictable expenses → lower deductible reduces uncertainty.

#### Indicative numbers (verify in official sources)
Many adults choose deductibles in a typical range (e.g., **CHF 300–2,500**). After the deductible, there is usually a cost‑sharing component (often **10%**) up to a yearly cap. There can also be additional contributions in hospital situations.

Use these as a mental framework and confirm current values with the FOPH (BAG/OFSP).

### 2.1) Choosing a deductible without complex math
A practical approach:
- If you can pay a high deductible without stress (and you don’t expect much care), it can make sense.
- If a “bad year” would break your budget, lower the deductible and buy predictability.

Optimise for **annual risk**, not for the cheapest monthly premium.

### 3) What you do when you’re sick (by model)
To avoid surprises, simulate day 1:
- **Standard**: you choose a doctor and book.
- **GP model**: you contact your GP first (except defined emergencies).
- **Telmed**: you call the hotline first and follow instructions.
- **HMO/network**: you contact your assigned centre/network.

If you know you won’t follow the pathway, don’t pick a “cheap” model that requires strict discipline.

### 3) Switching insurers: calendar and proof
Switching is usually prepared in autumn and has deadlines. Practical method:
- compare early,
- don’t leave it to the last day,
- keep written confirmations (cancellation/enrolment).

Rule of thumb: assume you may need to prove any important submission later. Save PDFs, emails and confirmations.

### 3.1) Moving: re‑compare in practice
Premiums depend on region. When you move canton/municipality:
- your premium can change,
- your HMO centre may become inconvenient,
- subsidies can change.

Recheck your setup after moving.

### 4) Premium subsidies (by canton)
Premium subsidies exist for certain income levels, but procedures and criteria vary by canton. If your budget is tight, check early: it can change your best model/deductible choice.

Practical tip: don’t wait until you’re “already struggling”. Subsidy processes can require documents and timelines.

### 5) Brokers/advisers: how to protect yourself
You can use advisers, but remember:
- basic insurance is regulated (benefits equal),
- transparency matters (who they represent, how they’re paid),
- your decision should be based on model rules and your profile, not “the cheapest month”.

If you’re pressured with artificial urgency (“only today”), treat it as a red flag. Basic insurance is a yearly decision; calm decisions are usually better decisions.

## Common mistakes
- Choosing purely on the lowest premium without understanding the model.
- High deductible without an emergency buffer.
- Buying supplementary “when I need it” (sometimes too late).
- Not reviewing accident cover (overpaying or under‑covering).
- Switching late without proof.
- Not re‑calculating after moving canton/municipality (premiums depend on region).
- Not keeping proof of cancellation/enrolment and later being unable to prove deadlines.

## Practical examples
### Example 1 — Healthy, disciplined and with a buffer
Profile: rare doctor visits, good process discipline.  
Coherent choice (depending on case): telmed or HMO + higher deductible.  
Risk: if you don’t follow model rules, savings disappear.

### Example 2 — Family with kids and frequent visits
Profile: paediatrics, urgent care, follow‑ups.  
Coherent choice: GP model (or standard if you need flexibility) + lower deductible for predictability.

### Example 3 — Moving within Switzerland
Change region and:
- premiums can change,
- an HMO centre may become inconvenient,
- subsidies may differ.

Method: notify the change, compare again, and rebuild your annual budget.

### Example 4 — Supplementary insurance: “I’ll buy it when I need it”
Common mistake: waiting until you need dental cover, private hospital room, or glasses and then shopping for supplementary insurance. In practice, many supplementary plans:
- require health questionnaires,
- may reject you or add exclusions.

If you think you’ll want it, compare **early**, while healthy.

## Useful advice
### A 20‑minute comparison method
1) Pick your model first (the one you will actually follow).  
2) Pick deductible based on risk and buffer.  
3) Compare in the official comparator with the same parameters.  
4) Look at annual cost and “service friction” (clear processes).  
5) Document everything (emails, confirmations).

### A quick decision matrix
Ask yourself:
- Am I disciplined with model rules (telmed/HMO)?
- Do I have a preferred doctor/centre?
- Do I have a buffer for a high deductible?
- Do I have predictable expenses (kids, chronic care, pregnancy, etc.)?

If you answer “no” to discipline or buffer, a more flexible model and/or a lower deductible usually fits better.

### Email questions template (keep answers in writing)
If you’re unsure, ask by email and save the reply:
- “Under my model, what steps must I follow to see a specialist?”
- “What counts as an emergency in your model?”
- “How can I confirm accident cover is excluded/included correctly?”

### Newcomer checklist
- Am I covered for accidents via my employer?
- What will I do tomorrow if I need a doctor (model rules)?
- Can I pay the deductible without stress?
- Do I want supplementary plans and should I compare early?
- Is a cantonal subsidy relevant for my budget?

### Mini glossary
- **LAMal/KVG**: mandatory basic insurance.  
- **Premium**: monthly payment.  
- **Deductible**: annual amount you pay first.  
- **Cost sharing**: what you pay after the deductible (up to a cap).  
- **Telmed/HMO/GP**: models with different rules.
