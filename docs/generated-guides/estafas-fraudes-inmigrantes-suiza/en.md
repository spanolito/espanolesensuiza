# Scams and fraud targeting immigrants in Switzerland: signals, real patterns and how to react

## Introduction
Switzerland is not immune to scams. Fraudsters adapt quickly to what works here: trust in institutions, “serious” communication, and newcomers who don’t yet know the system.

In immigrant communities, clear patterns repeat:
- calls that appear to come from the **police** (spoofing),
- the **callback** scam (“please call this +41 number urgently”),
- clean phishing (no typos, no weird links),
- rental/job scams and “compassion” scams.

This guide gives you a practical framework: how to recognise them, what to do in the moment, how to document, and where to report (noting that details can vary by canton).

## How the system works
### 1) Why scams work so well on newcomers
Scammers exploit:
- **urgency** (“do it now or consequences”),
- **authority** (police, bank, administration),
- **shame** (people don’t report).

In Switzerland there is a fourth factor: formal processes (letters, official‑looking references). Many scams imitate that tone.

### 2) Spoofing: the number you see can be fake
Phone calls can display an “official” number. That does not prove anything: caller ID can be **spoofed**.

Rule: don’t trust what appears on screen. Trust verification: you call back using a verified official number.

## Requirements or rules
### 1) Universal scam signals (checklist)
- pressure to act immediately,
- requests for sensitive data (codes, logins, PIN),
- requests to transfer money to “safe accounts”,
- attempts to isolate you (“don’t tell anyone”),
- the story changes when you ask details.

### 2) “Fake police” (spoofing) — common script
Pattern:
- your phone shows a real police number,
- they claim an investigation, debt or legal case,
- they push for personal data or money transfers “to protect it”.

What to do:
- hang up,
- find your cantonal police official number on an official site,
- call back yourself.

### 3) Callback scam — no links, still dangerous
Pattern:
- you receive an SMS/email “from your bank / TWINT / Amazon / PayPal / an authority”,
- it says “suspicious transaction—call this +41 number urgently”.

Trap: you call, and you reach fraudsters.

Rule: never call numbers in the message. Use the official app or the official number from the bank’s website/app.

### 4) Rental and job scams (very common for immigrants)
Renting:
- “no viewing”, price too good,
- upfront money without a verifiable contract,
- unclear identity.

Jobs:
- “instant contract” without a real interview,
- requests for money to “process” a contract/permit,
- companies with no verifiable footprint.

### 4.1) Fake invoices and “Swiss payment” traps (QR, IBAN, TWINT)
In Switzerland, paying by invoice and QR bills is common. That convenience is also exploited:
- you receive an “invoice” with a wrong beneficiary/IBAN,
- you’re pushed to pay via TWINT to an unknown number,
- or you scan a QR that leads to manipulated payment data.

Practical rules:
- verify the beneficiary (name/IBAN) and don’t pay under pressure,
- if the invoice is unexpected, confirm via the official channel (app/website/official phone),
- for rent/insurance/administration, compare with previous official communication.

### 4.2) “Administrative” scams (well‑written letters/emails)
Many people think scams have typos and weird links. Not anymore. You’ll see clean messages that imitate Swiss administrative tone.
What still signals fraud: urgency + request for data/payment + pressure to avoid verification.

### 5) “Compassion” scams
Emotional stories + pressure to transfer “today”. It can be real or fake, but the manipulation pattern is the same.

Practical rule: if you want to help, use verifiable channels (organisations, associations, traceable support), not impulsive transfers.

### 6) What a real bank or police will NOT ask you to do
As a practical rule, be suspicious if someone asks you to:
- share one‑time codes (OTP) or passwords,
- transfer money to “safe accounts”,
- install apps “to verify”,
- buy gift cards or crypto to “solve” an issue,
- stay on the phone while you execute payments.

Legitimate institutions allow verification through official channels and don’t need your codes.

### 7) Anti‑scam checklist for renting
- Verifiable viewing (or a trusted person views for you).
- Clear contract with coherent details.
- Traceable deposit (ideally a blocked deposit account).
- Verifiable counterpart (real agency/landlord).
- No “reservation payments” without proper documents.

### 8) Anti‑scam checklist for jobs
- Verifiable company (site, address, phone, footprint).
- Real hiring process (interview, professional exchange, coherent offer).
- Never pay to “process” a contract or permit.
- Contract with clear terms (salary, hours, start date).
- If sensitive data is requested, it must be via a secure and justified channel.

## Common mistakes
- “If the number is official, it must be real.”
- Calling the SMS number without verifying.
- Sharing one‑time codes (OTP) by phone.
- Transferring money “to protect it”.
- Accepting a rental without viewing/contract.
- Not reporting out of shame.
- Believing “if it looks Swiss and serious, it’s real”.
- Sending ID document photos without verifying who receives them.
- Paying via irreversible methods without verification.

## Practical examples
### Example 1 — “We are the police, your name is in a file”
Correct response in 30 seconds:
1) “Thank you, I’ll hang up and call the official number.”  
2) hang up.  
3) verify the number on the official site.  
4) call back yourself.  

### Example 2 — Bank SMS: “call urgently”
Response:
- don’t call the message number,
- open the official app or call the official number,
- if unsure, temporarily block card/account access.

### Example 3 — “Perfect” apartment, too cheap
Rule:
- no verified viewing + clear contract + traceable deposit = no transfer.

### Example 4 — “Verify with a code”
Pattern: you’re told “I sent you a code—tell it to me to verify”. That code is often a one‑time password to access banking or approve an action.

Rule: codes are for you, not for “verification” with a caller.

## Useful advice
### Self‑protection habits
- Enable 2FA on banking and email.
- Never share OTP/PIN.
- Save official numbers (bank, cantonal police, insurers).
- Be suspicious of urgency: serious institutions allow verification.
- If in doubt, ask for everything in writing and take time.

### A 30‑second protocol (works for most scams)
1) Pause: don’t act under urgency.  
2) Cut: hang up or don’t reply.  
3) Verify: you call back via an official channel.  
4) Document: save message, number, timestamp.  

### Reporting checklist (so it’s actually useful)
- screenshots of SMS/email,
- number that called (and displayed number),
- time/date,
- payment data (IBAN, TWINT, reference),
- what you did (transfer, shared data).

The clearer the report, the more useful it is for police and your bank.

### If you already fell for it
1) Contact your bank immediately (blocks/chargebacks).  
2) Save evidence: messages, emails, numbers, screenshots, transfers.  
3) Report via the cantonal police channel (procedures vary).  
4) Change passwords and review account access.

### What can vary by canton
- police contacts and portals,
- reporting procedures,
- local prevention resources.
